What the Seagulls Said

On the sand of Virginia’s old shore, I fell in love with the sea. I knew that I would bring my first love there. It’s where I would impress her with my manly prowess, diving into the water and emerging with nature’s bounty. She’d be blown away by such a primal display. We’d eat the blue crabs I had caught, sleep together in the sand under the stars, and fall in love. Every time she went to the beach or smelled crabs, she’d think of me. It was a simple, foolproof plan.

My dad spent part of his young adulthood as a crabber on a small boat. This was during the years before many of the families went extinct to larger companies and declining harvests. In the same way, my grandparents and their parents before them built their legacies by the sea on the Korean peninsula.

I spent all my summers there, by the sea. It’s where I felt most at home. As I got older, I would bring my closest friends. We laughed at imaginary shapes in the clouds. Our throats burned from swallowing brackish water and shouting our favorite songs in the car.

The water in this pocket of the bay was warm and clear. We waded in and talked about what directions life would take us in the future. Between the silences, the ripples of our reflections on the surface carried conversations of their own. There was another world parallel to our own that spoke while we were quiet, saving us from awkward silences. Sand and salt dried in our hair, making it stiff and coarse.

I have a particular memory that glows in my mind. It glows so brightly that I can feel it burning my skull. We were only 17 years old, then. I was taking photos on a film camera I had purchased for $5. Whenever I turned to face my friends, they smiled not at my camera, but at me. My stomach twisted and I felt my throat catch. What was said meant nothing those days. We only cared about what was meant. We hid nothing from each other. We were deafened by the wind.

The fun of planning for the future was that we knew it would all change anyway. The tightness of my sunburnt cheeks stung, but the sunset’s warmth on my face made up for it. We laughed about the ridiculousness of it all.

Decades later, and on the other side of the country, I still feel my blood pulling me to the old coast. Every so often, a seagull will ride a stray, salty gust from the sea travelling inland. It’ll caw at me as if to say,

"Come home.”
